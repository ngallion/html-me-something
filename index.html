Tea Bagging

Since a young age, I have enjoyed the fruits of the revered herb Camellia Sinensis. Up until age 20, I knew it only as the crushed-up leaves in a piece of folded-up and stapled filter paper (tea bag). My first favorite tea was the ever-popular Earl Grey, which I would brew in a coffee cup in the microwave (for one minute) and add roughly 25-50% cold milk, and almost as much granulated sugar.

I dabbled with other types--ginger to ease the stomach, green for a light taste, and various other fruit infused varieties. But, for the first part of my life, when I thought of tea, I imagined the taste of sugary, milky earl grey. They were simpler times.

The Reign of Coffee

Beginning sometime around age 18, I was introduced to the miraculous wonders of the coffee bean. While coffee is certainly not tea, I would consider it a stop on my "voyage" into tea. I began with sugared up Starbucks concoctions, but gradually descended into home brewing--the deeper I descended, the darker my cups turned, until I was drinking pure, unadulturated, black bean water (which is still my preference). Eventually I found myself making americanos with an Aeropress every morning before work. Coffee holds a special place in my heart, but I've found that it is quite the double-edged sword. Caffeine dependency is no joke. I found myself relying too much on its abilities to wake me up, so I decided to take a break and pursue other, less potent forms of alertness enablers.

Loosening Up

I took some time off coffee and explored other avenues of caffeine administration. I stumbled across an old ball diffuser I had received as a gift a few years prior, and looked into loose leaf teas. I tried a few black teas, hoping for a higher caffeine content over greens and tisanes (tea-talk for fruit infusions). After browsing several local stores, I found a few brands I liked, and a few I didn't (looking at you, Lipton). Eventually, I took my shopping online and found some excellent brands (shout-out to Upton Tea Imports). Earl Grey was a mainstay for a while, but I also explored teas from Yunnan and Darjeeling. I began to wonder where all these fancy names came from! After some researching, I unearthed a wealth of internet knowledge regarding different tea locales, along with various forms of "oxidation"--whatever that meant. I continued to explore these different origins, though stuck entirely to black/highly oxidized leaves. Eventually I evolved from the ball diffuser to a rustic tea pot, which allows the leaves to breathe more and diffuse more elegant flavors. But my obsession with the sacred shrub was only beginning.
